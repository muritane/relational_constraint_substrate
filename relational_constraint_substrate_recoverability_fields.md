# Recoverability Support Fields

## Localized Recovery, Regeneration Domains, Support Density, and the Spatial Distribution of Distinctions

---

# Status

This document is a companion to:

```text
Relational Constraint Substrate
Formal Relational Constraint Substrate
```

The purpose of this document is narrower.

It refines the role of recoverability by making explicit that recoverability is not uniformly distributed across reality.

A distinction may be highly recoverable in one region, weakly recoverable in another, and unrecoverable elsewhere.

The central revision is:

> Distinctions do not merely exist or fail to exist. They have support distributions across substrates, regions, projections, costs, horizons, and regeneration mechanisms.

In compressed form:

> A distinction persists where it can be recovered or regenerated at admissible cost.

---

# Abstract

The formal substrate defined recoverability as decoder-indexed recovery of projected distinctions over a horizon, at bounded cost, error, and probability.

This document adds locality.

Recoverability is indexed not only by:

```text
projection
resolution
decoder
horizon
cost
probability
use-context
```

but also by:

```text
region
substrate
carrier density
access path
regeneration mechanism
resistance gradient
```

A dollar distinction is highly recoverable inside banks, payment systems, ledgers, contracts, human practices, and Federal Reserve infrastructure.

It is weakly recoverable in remote wilderness.

It is essentially unrecoverable near the Earth's core unless a supporting representation is carried there.

An electron distinction, by contrast, is recoverable across far broader physical domains because the constraint regimes that regenerate electron-relevant distinctions are not dependent on human institutional substrates.

This does not mean the electron is metaphysically more real in a separate category.

It means its recovery field is supported by different, broader, and more fundamental constraint regimes.

In compressed form:

> The difference between an electron, a dollar, a corporation, a language, a roof leak, and a private memory is not whether they are physical. All require physical instantiation. The difference is how their recoverability is distributed, regenerated, and resisted across substrates.

# 1. Local Recoverability

## 1.1 Definition

A distinction `D(x, y)` is locally recoverable in region `L` under projection `π_i` when a decoder `Δ` can recover the distinction from traces, carriers, or interactions available within `L`.

Formally:

```text
LocalRec(D; L, π_i, Δ, H, C, ε, p, U)
```

holds when:

```text
P[ recovery of D from substrate traces in L by Δ within H at cost ≤ C and error ≤ ε ] ≥ p
```

In compressed form:

> A distinction is locally real to an agent when the local substrate contains enough recoverable structure to reconstruct it for the relevant use-context.

---

## 1.2 Recovery Field

For each distinction `D`, define a recovery field:

```text
Φ_D : L → ℝ_{0}
```

where:

```text
Φ_D(L)
```

measures the recoverability strength of `D` in region `L`.

High values mean:

```text
low recovery cost
high recovery probability
many independent carriers
many available projections
short recovery horizon
low decoder complexity
```

Low values mean:

```text
high recovery cost
low probability
few or no carriers
weak projection access
long reconstruction path
high decoder complexity
```

In compressed form:

> A distinction has a recoverability landscape, not merely a location.

---

## 1.3 Local Unrecoverability

A distinction may be globally supported but locally unrecoverable.

For example:

```text
D = dollar distinction
```

may be recoverable in:

```text
banks
payment systems
legal records
contracts
human practices
central bank infrastructure
```

but unrecoverable in:

```text
uninhabited deep ocean rock
mantle depth
near Earth core
interplanetary vacuum without carried records
```

This does not mean the dollar distinction does not exist anywhere.

It means the local region lacks the carriers, decoders, or interaction paths required for recovery.

In compressed form:

> Global persistence does not imply local recoverability.

# 2. Support Regions

## 2.1 Recovery Support

The support region of distinction `D` at threshold `θ` is:

```text
Supp_θ(D) = { L | Φ_D(L) ≥ θ }
```

This is the set of regions where `D` can be recovered above the required threshold.

The threshold may depend on:

```text
use-context
agent capability
cost budget
time horizon
risk tolerance
projection family
```

In compressed form:

> A support region is the domain where a distinction remains operationally recoverable.

---

## 2.2 Support Is Not Mere Spatial Occupancy

A support region is not always identical to geometric containment.

A corporation may have:

```text
legal support
financial support
employee support
infrastructure support
computational support
brand support
```

distributed across many regions.

A scientific concept may have:

```text
textbook support
laboratory support
instrumental support
training support
mathematical support
```

spread across institutions and archives.

A biological species may have:

```text
genetic support
ecosystem support
reproductive support
behavioral support
fossil support
```

across different horizons.

In compressed form:

> The support of a distinction is the distribution of its recoverability paths, not merely the volume occupied by a bounded object.

---

## 2.3 Finite Support

Many distinctions have finite support.

The dollar does not extend indefinitely into:

```text
Earth's core
interstellar vacuum
regions with no human records
regions with no institutional coupling
```

unless supporting traces are transported there.

The support region of a human institution is therefore finite and substrate-dependent.

In compressed form:

> Institutional distinctions occupy the regions where their traces, agents, procedures, and enforcement paths remain recoverable.

# 3. Recovery Density

## 3.1 Definition

Recovery density measures how much distinction-supporting structure exists in a region.

Let:

```text
σ_D(L)
```

represent the density of recoverability carriers for distinction `D` in region `L`.

Carriers may include:

```text
physical marks
records
brains
servers
contracts
signals
instruments
habits
procedures
schemas
measurement devices
training distributions
regulatory mechanisms
```

In compressed form:

> Recovery density is the local concentration of structures that make a distinction reconstructible.

---

## 3.2 Hubs

A hub is a region of unusually high recovery density.

For the dollar distinction, hubs include:

```text
Federal Reserve systems
commercial banks
payment networks
financial databases
legal institutions
accounting systems
currency exchanges
```

The Federal Reserve building is not itself the dollar.

But it may be a high-density support hub for dollar-relevant distinctions.

In compressed form:

> A hub is not the object; it is a region where many recovery paths converge.

---

## 3.3 Redundancy

A distinction becomes more persistent when it is redundantly recoverable across many substrates.

For example:

```text
single memory in one brain
```

has low redundancy.

```text
written note + digital backup + shared convention + institutional procedure
```

has higher redundancy.

Redundancy increases:

```text
persistence probability
resistance to carrier loss
recovery after disruption
transmission across horizons
```

In compressed form:

> Redundancy converts fragile distinction into persistent organization.

# 4. Regeneration Domains

## 4.1 Definition

A regeneration domain is a region or substrate regime that does not merely store a distinction, but actively recreates it.

Let:

```text
Regen(D; L)
```

mean that region `L` contains processes that regenerate distinction `D` over time.

Examples:

```text
legal systems regenerate corporation distinctions
markets regenerate price distinctions
schools regenerate language distinctions
laboratories regenerate scientific distinctions
oscillators regenerate clock distinctions
physical law regenerates electron-relevant distinctions
```

In compressed form:

> Storage preserves a distinction; regeneration recreates it.

---

## 4.2 Storage Versus Regeneration

A stored distinction can decay if no process refreshes it.

A regenerated distinction can persist across carrier turnover.

For example:

```text
forgotten document in archive
```

may store a distinction but fail to regenerate it in active use.

```text
living legal practice
```

regenerates distinctions through:

```text
interpretation
recognition
enforcement
procedure
training
record maintenance
```

In compressed form:

> A distinction lives longer when it is regenerated, not merely recorded.

---

## 4.3 Regeneration and Reality Mapping

Some distinctions are regenerated by physical constraint regimes.

Examples:

```text
electron charge
chemical bond
mass-energy relation
thermal gradient
```

Other distinctions are regenerated by biological, cognitive, institutional, or computational regimes.

Examples:

```text
species category
word meaning
corporation
currency
software interface
scientific model
```

This does not create a physical/nonphysical split.

It creates a distinction between regeneration regimes.

In compressed form:

> The question is not whether a distinction is physical, but which constraint regimes regenerate it.

# 5. Resistance Gradients

## 5.1 Recovery Cost Gradient

The recovery cost of a distinction may increase with distance from its support hubs.

Define:

```text
Cost_D(L, Δ, U)
```

as the cost of recovering distinction `D` in region `L` using decoder `Δ` for use-context `U`.

Then a resistance gradient is the variation of this cost across regions.

Low resistance:

```text
many carriers
many decoders
high redundancy
high institutional recognition
low access cost
```

High resistance:

```text
few traces
hostile substrate
missing decoder
long causal distance
weak institutional coupling
high energy requirement
```

In compressed form:

> Resistance is the cost of forcing a distinction to become recoverable where the substrate does not already support it.

---

## 5.2 Examples

Recovering the dollar distinction in a bank:

```text
low cost
high probability
many decoders
```

Recovering the dollar distinction in a cave with no artifacts:

```text
high cost
low local support
requires transported representation or external communication
```

Recovering the dollar distinction near the Earth's core:

```text
locally near impossible
requires extraordinary energy and transported support
```

Recovering solar radiation in interplanetary vacuum:

```text
low cost if proper detector exists
```

Recovering Federal Reserve authority in interplanetary vacuum:

```text
not locally supported unless human institutional traces or communication channels are present
```

In compressed form:

> Different distinctions have different recovery geographies.

---

## 5.3 Imposed Support

A distinction can be made recoverable in a hostile region by transporting or constructing support.

Examples:

```text
spacecraft carries clocks, ledgers, protocols, maps, currency records
underwater station carries human legal and computational systems
server farm extends institutional distinctions into remote territory
probe carries Earth-origin measurement conventions into space
```

The distinction is not recovered from the local vacuum alone.

It is recovered from the support structure transported into that region.

In compressed form:

> Expansion of a distinction's domain requires either local regeneration or imported support.

---

If not transmitted, stored, or acted upon, their persistence horizon may be bounded by the carrier's lifetime.

In compressed form:

# 8. Boundaries as Recoverability Gradients

## 8.1 Boundary

A boundary is not always a sharp surface.

It may be a gradient in recoverability, coupling, enforcement, cost, or projection access.

For distinction `D`, a boundary may be defined as a region where:

```text
Φ_D(L)
```

falls below operational threshold.

In compressed form:

> A boundary is where a distinction stops being cheaply recoverable.

---

## 8.2 Institutional Boundary

A corporation's boundary is not only the walls of its buildings.

It includes thresholds where its distinctions cease to be locally recognized or enforceable.

Examples:

```text
employee vs non-employee
asset vs non-asset
contractually bound vs not bound
inside network vs outside network
jurisdictionally recognized vs not recognized
```

In compressed form:

> Institutional boundaries are recoverability and enforcement boundaries, not merely spatial edges.

---

## 8.3 Physical Boundary

Physical objects also have recoverability boundaries.

A rock, storm, electron cloud, organism, or galaxy may not have perfectly sharp boundaries under every projection.

The boundary depends on:

```text
projection
resolution
interaction strength
measurement threshold
use-context
```

In compressed form:

> Physical boundaries are also projection-relative, though often supported by strong natural constraint gradients.

# 9. Distinction Discovery as Support Expansion

## 9.1 Discovery

A distinction is discovered by an agent when a new projection exposes a previously unavailable partition of state space.

```text
Π_old → Π_old ∪ {π_new}
```

This can expand the effective state space:

```text
X / ~_{Π_old}
```

to a finer operational partition:

```text
X / ~_{Π_old ∪ {π_new}}
```

In compressed form:

> Discovery refines the agent's operational ontology.

---

## 9.2 Discovery Creates New Local Recoverability

A new distinction does not automatically become globally recoverable.

It initially exists where the projection, decoder, and supporting carriers exist.

Example:

```text
roof collapse
```

may initially be the only recognized distinction.

Then a finer projection exposes:

```text
minor leak
moisture accumulation
wood rot
load weakening
pre-collapse deformation
```

This creates earlier intervention paths.

In compressed form:

> Early detection moves catastrophic distinction backward into recoverable precursor states.

---

## 9.3 Preemptive Intervention

Projection refinement can reduce the need for reactive action.

If a system can recover precursor distinctions before failure, then it can change reachable futures.

Formally:

```text
π_new exposes D_precursor
```

and:

```text
A_K(system | D_precursor) includes repair paths
```

that were not available under the older projection.

In compressed form:

> Observability expands action space before catastrophe becomes the only recoverable signal.

> The distinction is not physical versus nonphysical; it is support structure and regeneration regime.

# 12. Effective State Space and Reality Approximation

## 12.1 Effective State Space

An agent does not operate on full reality state space `X`.

It operates on the quotient exposed by its projections:

```text
X_eff = X / ~_Π
```

If no distinctions are available, the effective state space collapses.

With each new recoverable distinction, the effective state space refines.

In compressed form:

> The world available to an agent is the world partitioned by its recoverable projections.

---

## 12.2 Learning as Projection Refinement

Learning can be modeled as discovering or constructing projections that preserve more task-relevant distinctions.

```text
Π_t → Π_{t+1}
```

where:

```text
Π_{t+1}
```

refines operational distinctions and changes reachable actions.

In compressed form:

> Learning expands the agent's effective world by making new distinctions recoverable.

---

## 12.3 Neural Networks

A neural network approximates functions over available data.

In this framework, it can also be viewed as learning a projection that preserves distinctions useful for an objective.

```text
f_hat(x) ≈ f(x)
```

but also:

```text
representation preserves distinctions relevant to loss minimization
```

It does not recover full reality.

It compresses, preserves, and transforms distinctions under a task regime.

In compressed form:

> A learned model is a projection-transform system optimized to preserve some distinctions and collapse others.

A concept survives when the distinction it encodes remains recoverable or regenerated across sufficient horizons.

```text
ConceptSurvives(D) ⇔ Regen(D) or Rec(D) remains above threshold over H
```

Concepts that fail to map onto any stable constraint regime may decay unless sustained by artificial support.

In compressed form:

> Concepts live while their distinctions remain recoverable, useful, or regenerated.

Concepts that map well to stable physical structure often survive because reality repeatedly regenerates their evidence.

In compressed form:

# 18. Candidate Measures

```text
Φ_D(L) = f(p, C, ε, H, n_carriers, n_decoders, redundancy, access_cost)
```

where high `Φ_D(L)` means easier recovery.

---

## 18.2 Support Volume

```text
Vol_θ(D) = measure({ L | Φ_D(L) ≥ θ })
```

This estimates how widely a distinction is recoverable above threshold.

---

## 18.3 Hub Density

```text
Hub_D(L) = local maximum of Φ_D(L)
```

A hub is a region where recoverability concentration is high relative to surrounding regions.

---

## 18.4 Regeneration Rate

```text
γ_D(L) = rate at which processes in L recreate recoverable traces of D
```

High regeneration rate means the distinction can survive carrier turnover.

---

## 18.5 Collapse Risk

```text
CollapseRisk(D, L, H) increases when:
```

```text
carrier redundancy decreases
decoder availability decreases
regeneration rate decreases
recovery cost increases
constraint support weakens
communication channels fail
```

In compressed form:

> Persistence can be modeled as the balance between decay, regeneration, redundancy, and recovery cost.

# 19. Failure Modes

## 19.1 Local Recovery Failure

A distinction is globally supported but unavailable in a local region.

```text
Φ_D(L) < θ
```

Example:

```text
dollar distinction near Earth's core
```

---

## 19.2 Hub Failure

A high-density support region is disrupted.

Example:

```text
central bank record failure
major database corruption
laboratory loss
archive destruction
```

The distinction may survive if redundancy is sufficient.

---

## 19.3 Channel Failure

A remote region loses access to a support hub.

Example:

```text
payment terminal disconnected from banking network
spacecraft loses communication with Earth
remote office loses access to corporate servers
```

---

## 19.4 Regeneration Failure

Processes that recreate the distinction stop operating.

Example:

```text
school stops teaching language
institution stops enforcing category
software protocol no longer maintained
```

---

## 19.5 Decoder Failure

Carriers remain but no system can interpret them.

Example:

```text
records exist but no one can read them
model weights exist but runtime is unavailable
sensor data exists but calibration is lost
```

---

## 19.6 Support Overextension

A distinction is projected into a region without adequate local support.

Example:

```text
legal rule asserted where no enforcement path exists
currency introduced without payment infrastructure
software deployed without runtime dependencies
```

In compressed form:

> A distinction fails when its recovery field falls below the demands placed on it.

---

# 21. Summary

Recoverability is not uniform.

A distinction has a distribution across regions, substrates, carriers, projections, costs, horizons, and regeneration mechanisms.

Some distinctions are broadly regenerated by physical constraint regimes.

Some are locally regenerated by institutions, organisms, software, cultures, or machines.

Some are private and die with their carrier unless transmitted.

A dollar is not recoverable from arbitrary vacuum or deep Earth material.

It is recoverable where the supporting institutional and informational substrate exists, or where that substrate has been transported.

A Federal Reserve building is not the dollar itself.

It is a high-density support hub inside the dollar's recovery field.

Other banks, ledgers, contracts, humans, servers, and payment systems increase the field's extent and redundancy.

In final compressed form:

```text
distinction
+ local carriers
+ decoders
+ regeneration processes
+ admissible access paths
= recoverability field
```

and:

```text
high recovery density
+ redundancy
+ regeneration
+ low resistance
= persistent support region
```

The central shift is:

```text
from asking where an object is
```

to:

```text
asking where its identity-relevant distinctions are recoverable, at what cost, by which projections, and through which substrates
```
